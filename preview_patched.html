Today's Briefing

AI & Technology

Cloudflare just eliminated 1,100 roles through AI and posted record revenue in the same reporting period.

That combination — fewer workers, more output — is the equation every CFO is now running.

It is not a warning sign.

It is the new template for how technology companies, and eventually all industries, will restructure their headcount.

OpenAI pushed new voice intelligence features into its developer API this week, cutting the cost and complexity of building real-time voice agents.

Enterprise adoption is accelerating across every function: back-office processing, compliance, customer service, and legal review.

Intel's AI hardware comeback is more dramatic than the headlines suggest, with the chip giant clawing back relevance against Nvidia in a data centre market being rewritten by AI workload demand.

A new small cybersecurity model published on Hugging Face — CyberSecQwen-4B — signals that AI security tooling is maturing out of research labs and into operational use.

Australian enterprises not yet mapping their AI exposure are already behind.

Australian Business & Finance

Australia's regional housing crisis has been compounding for 25 years, and advocates say this federal budget must confront it or lose another generation of essential workers from regional communities.

Teachers, nurses, and tradespeople are being priced out of the towns that need them most.

Separately, a gap in Australian superannuation law is leaving domestic workers — including many nannies — without compulsory employer contributions.

The exemption disproportionately hits women in lower-income roles and quietly widens the retirement savings deficit over decades.

It is a fixable oversight that has waited too long.

Australians living with motor neurone disease are raising urgent alarms about proposed NDIS changes that could strip fast-tracked support — access that, for people with rapidly deteriorating conditions, is the difference between independence and crisis.

The Coalition's electric vehicle policy is also under pressure, with critics arguing its fringe benefits tax position looks disconnected from where consumers and businesses are already heading.

World Markets & Global Business

Oil prices jumped after US and Iranian forces exchanged fire in the Strait of Hormuz — the waterway through which roughly 20% of global oil supply passes every day.

Iran called the action a "reckless military adventure." Any sustained disruption pushes fuel and shipping costs higher for Australian importers, adding fresh pressure to households already stretched by the cost of living.

On the diplomatic front, Trump announced a three-day Russia-Ukraine ceasefire, though military analysts remain deeply sceptical — similar agreements have collapsed before, and Ukrainian infrastructure continues to take damage regardless of political announcements.

A significant international cyberattack disrupted university and school networks across multiple countries, exposing persistent vulnerabilities in education sector digital infrastructure.

Australian institutions should be asking hard questions about their own readiness right now.

The Big Picture

Two forces are competing for the shape of the next decade, and today's news makes both visible.

First: AI is making companies more profitable while making them structurally smaller.

Cloudflare's numbers are not a one-off — the same arithmetic will reach Australian professional services, financial institutions, logistics operators, and government agencies.

The timeline is shorter than most executives believe.

Second: geopolitical instability is making every assumption harder to hold.

Rising oil prices from the Strait of Hormuz, a hantavirus outbreak spreading through international cruise passengers, a fragile Ukrainian ceasefire, and growing Middle East escalation risk all create conditions where cost planning and supply chain assumptions become unreliable.

The businesses that outperform will be those with flexibility built into their operations, not those optimised for a stable world that no longer exists.

The workers who will be fine are those building skills AI cannot replicate on demand.

The window is open.

It will not stay that way.
